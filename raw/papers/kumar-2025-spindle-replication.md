# Closed-loop auditory stimulation increases sleep spindle density but does not improve memory consolidation: a multi-site replication attempt

## Abstract

Rodriguez et al. (2024) reported that closed-loop auditory stimulation during sleep enhanced spindle density and improved declarative memory consolidation (d=0.68). Given the therapeutic implications, we conducted a pre-registered, multi-site replication across three laboratories (London, Tokyo, Toronto) with a larger sample and two memory tasks. 204 participants (age 20-38) were randomized to stimulation (n=102) or sham (n=102) in a double-blind design. Stimulation successfully increased spindle density by 24% (d=0.88, p<0.001), replicating the physiological effect. However, stimulation did not significantly improve word-pair recall (68% vs 65%, p=0.22, d=0.18) or face-name recall (71% vs 69%, p=0.41, d=0.12). Bayesian analyses provided moderate evidence for the null hypothesis on memory outcomes (BF01=4.2 for word-pairs, BF01=6.1 for face-names). Exploratory subgroup analyses revealed a significant stimulation × baseline spindle density interaction: participants with low baseline spindle density showed a memory benefit (d=0.52, p=0.03), while those with average or high baseline density did not. These results suggest that the spindle-memory link may be more nuanced than initially reported, with stimulation effects potentially limited to individuals with suboptimal baseline spindle activity.

## Results

### Physiological Effect (Replicated)

Stimulation increased spindle density by 24% vs sham (p<0.001, d=0.88), consistent with Rodriguez et al. (28%, d=0.95). No differences in total sleep time, sleep efficiency, or time in any sleep stage. The closed-loop stimulation reliably enhances spindles across sites.

### Memory Effect (Not Replicated)

| Task | Stimulation | Sham | p | d | BF01 |
|------|------------|------|---|---|------|
| Word-pair recall (24h) | 68% | 65% | 0.22 | 0.18 | 4.2 |
| Face-name recall (24h) | 71% | 69% | 0.41 | 0.12 | 6.1 |

The original study reported d=0.68 for word-pair recall. Our point estimate (d=0.18) falls well below this. Bayesian analysis favored the null hypothesis 4-6x over the alternative.

### Subgroup Analysis (Exploratory)

Dividing participants by median baseline spindle density:

| Subgroup | Stimulation recall | Sham recall | d | p |
|----------|-------------------|-------------|---|---|
| Low baseline spindles (n=102) | 72% | 64% | 0.52 | 0.03 |
| High baseline spindles (n=102) | 65% | 66% | -0.06 | 0.81 |

The interaction was significant (p=0.01). This suggests a ceiling effect: individuals who already have adequate spindle density may not benefit from further enhancement.

### Mediation Analysis

Unlike Rodriguez (2024), spindle density increase did not significantly mediate memory outcomes in the full sample (indirect effect p=0.31). In the low-baseline subgroup only, partial mediation was observed (38% mediated, p=0.04), compared to Rodriguez's 62%.

## Limitations

- Exploratory subgroup analysis was not pre-registered — should be treated as hypothesis-generating
- Different stimulation hardware across sites (though protocols were matched)
- Age range (20-38) still restricted to young adults
- Two declarative memory tasks — procedural memory not tested
- The original Rodriguez (2024) study's lead author is a co-author here, reducing concerns about protocol divergence but introducing potential bias
