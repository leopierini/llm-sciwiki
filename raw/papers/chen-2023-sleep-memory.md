# Sleep spindle density predicts memory consolidation in healthy adults

## Abstract

Sleep spindles — bursts of 12-15 Hz oscillatory activity during non-REM sleep — have been implicated in memory consolidation. We investigated whether spindle density during a post-learning nap predicts subsequent recall of word-pair associations. 120 healthy adults (age 20-35) learned 80 word pairs, then either napped for 90 minutes (n=60) or stayed awake (n=60). Recall was tested immediately and after 24 hours.

## Results

Nap group showed significantly better 24-hour retention than wake group (72% vs 58%, p<0.001, d=0.81). Within the nap group, spindle density over centroparietal electrodes correlated with 24-hour recall improvement (r=0.43, p<0.001). The effect remained significant after controlling for baseline memory performance, age, and total sleep time (beta=0.38, p=0.002).

Spindle density did not predict immediate recall (r=0.08, p=0.54), suggesting the effect is specific to consolidation rather than encoding.

## Limitations

- Young adult sample only — may not generalize to older adults where spindle activity declines
- Word-pair task is one type of declarative memory; unclear if results extend to procedural or emotional memory
- Correlational design within nap group — spindle density was not experimentally manipulated
- Single nap session — unclear if the effect is stable across nights
